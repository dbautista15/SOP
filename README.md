# SOP for Grad School
My statement of Purpose
The more time I spend as a software engineer, the more I believe that the discipline is
fundamentally about one thing: data. How we structure it, how we move it, how we scale systems
to handle more of it, how we ensure it remains accessible, secure, and intact. Every meaningful
engineering problem I have encountered - from designing database schemas to building real-
time synchronization systems to optimizing processing pipelines - has ultimately been a question
of data at its core. Machine learning is where that question becomes something more powerful.
It is no longer just about handling data well. It is about learning from it, and about what richer,
more intelligent systems become possible when you do. That realization is what brought me to
pursue a Master of Science in Computer Science at UNC Charlotte, and it is the lens through which
I intend to do the work.
My undergraduate curriculum gave me the mathematical and technical foundation to pursue this
seriously. Coursework in Engineering Linear Algebra, Probability and Statistics, and Artificial
Intelligence gave me the theoretical grounding behind how machine learning systems are built -
from understanding high-dimensional representations and similarity metrics to reasoning about
model behavior and performance. Beyond the classroom, I developed systems thinking through
projects that required me to move from concept to production under real constraints. I built
SmartSeater, a real-time table management system deployed in a live restaurant environment,
where I designed backtracking and memoization algorithms to optimize seat assignments across
128 seats and implemented Socket.io for concurrent staff synchronization. In a separate
hackathon, I built Introspect - an offline, privacy-first journaling application powered by a local
ML model using sentence-transformer embeddings and cosine similarity, optimized to run
entirely on-device with no cloud dependency. These projects taught me what it means to think
about architecture, performance, and real-world constraints at the same time.
What I want to develop in graduate school is the depth to build at the intersection of software
engineering and machine learning - not just as a practitioner who applies existing tools, but as an
engineer who understands the systems underneath them well enough to extend and improve
them. I am drawn to questions about how intelligent systems are architected for real-world
deployment: how models are made efficient, how they are integrated into larger software
systems, and how data pipelines are designed to feed them well. I am open to where research
takes me within this space. What I know is that the engineers building the most capable and
meaningful applications of the next decade will need to understand both the software systems
layer and the machine learning layer with equal fluency, and closing that gap is exactly what I am
here to do.
My professional goal is to work as a software engineer at a company operating at the frontier of
these technologies - building systems where intelligent software meets real scale. I want to be
the kind of engineer who does not just use machine learning as a feature, but understands it
deeply enough to make architectural decisions around it, to know when it is the right tool and
how to deploy it responsibly. This past year I had the opportunity to interview with my dream company for a
summer internship - an experience that clarified for me not just where I want to go, but what it
will take to get there. Engineers operating at that level work at the intersection of large-scale
systems and machine learning with genuine depth and fluency. The master's program is how I
develop that - not to meet a requirement, but to become the engineer those opportunities
demand. A master's degree is not a credential I am pursuing for its own sake. It is the difference
between an engineer who can implement and one who can design. I want to be the latter.
I want to be transparent about my undergraduate record, because the context matters. I am
bilingual and the first in my family to attend college. I entered university without a roadmap - no
one in my household had navigated financial aid appeals, grade replacement policies, or
academic advising in the way that students with that background often take for granted. For
much of my undergraduate career I was also working two jobs. There were semesters where
keeping the lights on meant sacrificing study time I could not afford to lose. I was not thriving
academically during those periods. I was surviving. And being first-generation had instilled in me
a habit of not asking for help - a reflex that served me in some parts of life and quietly worked
against me in others.
The shift came in Professor Jacob Krevat's Network Based Applications course, where students
competed to build a complete full-stack application from the ground up. I put everything I had
into that project - and I won. That win did something I had not anticipated: it dismantled my
imposter syndrome. It showed me in concrete terms that when I commit fully to the work, I am
capable of competing at the highest level. From that moment I stopped surviving my education
and started owning it. I found a genuine passion for software engineering - for the architecture
of it, for the elegance of a well-designed system, for the satisfaction of solving hard problems
well. I also began to unlearn the habit of isolation. I started asking for help, building relationships
with professors and peers, and learning how to use the full resources of an academic
environment. That shift in mindset changed everything.
Graduate school represents something specific to me: the opportunity to do this right, from the
beginning, with full intention. No survival mode. No divided attention. I know what I am studying
and why. I know the kind of engineer I am working to become. The version of me entering this
program is not the same person who entered undergrad without a map - it is someone who found
the passion, proved he could compete, and is now ready to go deep. I have also spent the past
two years making sure the next first-generation student has a better experience than I did,
volunteering as a Tech Fellow and mentor across Code-Path, the Upward Math Science Program,
and the PAAL Boys and Girls Club. I mentor because I know exactly what it costs a student not to
have someone in their corner who knows the terrain.
UNC Charlotte shaped who I am as an engineer. I would be honored to continue that formation
as a graduate student in the Department of Computer Science - this time, with everything I have
learned about what it means to show up fully for the work.
